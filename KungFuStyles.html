Difference between Northern style Kung Fu and Southern style Kung Fu


Northern style Kung Fu

Kung Fu styles originating from areas north of the Yangtze River are commonly referred to as Northern Kung Fu or "long fist" style. This form of Kung Fu is one of the older martial arts systems, distinguished by its

practitioners' historically better nutrition and taller stature, particularly in China's northern, more mountainous regions. This contributed to an emphasis on high kicks. The stances in Northern Kung Fu are generally more

vertical and are often seen as more elegant and graceful, incorporating movements that border on the acrobatic.

However, it's important not to misconceive Northern Kung Fu as merely ornamental. Despite its more upright posture and aesthetically pleasing techniques, it retains a high level of athleticism and power.

Examples of Northern style Kung Fu

  • Northern Shaolin Kung Fu


  • Baguazhang


  • Bajiquan


  • Tai Chi


Southern style Kung Fu

Southern Kung Fu, which emerged from the warmer and more varied landscapes of southern China, is characterized by its fluid and elegant movements, often integrating elements of dance and acrobatics. This style is

known for utilizing a diverse array of weapons, including staffs and spears. Developed more recently than its northern counterpart, Southern Kung Fu is recognized for its lower, more assertive stances and a stronger focus

on punching. When it comes to kicking, practitioners typically aim lower, targeting areas like the groin. The rationale behind these lower stances and kicks is to maximize power. Short, low kicks are effective in conserving

movement while allowing for rapid, targeted attacks on vulnerable areas like the groin, neck, and eyes. Contrasting with the more upright nature of Northern Kung Fu, the Southern style is perceived as less aesthetically

pleasing by some. However, it is particularly suited for close-range combat, making it a practical and powerful martial arts system


Examples of Southern style Kung Fu

  • Choy Li Fut


  • Wing Chun


  • Hung Gar


  • Baimei Fist


What are the cultural and historical differences between theses styles?

Several cultural and historical elements contribute to the distinctions between Northern and Southern Kung Fu styles. Southern China's landscape, with its dense forests, swamps, and uneven, muddy grounds, starkly

contrasts with the open terrains of the North. This region also faced more threats from wildlife and was more susceptible to attacks by robbers and pirates. Given the generally shorter stature of individuals in the South,

Southern Kung Fu appears to have evolved primarily as a means of self-defense, adapted from Northern techniques to suit the more challenging southern environment. On the other hand, some view the less densely

populated North as a region more vulnerable to banditry. This might explain the Northern preference for weapon usage. Northern Kung Fu practitioners often relied on weapons, and their unarmed techniques were possibly

more focused on training and demonstration purposes. In contrast, the Southern population typically didn't carry weapons, and when they did, these were smaller and more concealable. This likely influenced Southern Kung

Fu to emphasize unarmed combat skills that were straightforward, potent, and efficient for quick incapacitation, somewhat resembling Krav Maga, the modern Israeli martial art. Additionally, it's theorized that Southern

Kung Fu was shaped by the necessities of shipboard combat, where a lower stance is advantageous on unstable surfaces and in confined spaces. An example of a martial art that evolved specifically from naval combat in

China is Wing Chun.


Which style is the best?

Ultimately, the decision of which Kung Fu style to pursue depends on your personal preference. Typically, Northern styles are recognized for their robust, straightforward movements and impactful strikes. In contrast,

Southern styles are celebrated for their smooth, elegant movements, with a focus on quickness and flexibility. It's important to note that both Northern and Southern Kung Fu styles are steeped in rich history and tradition.

Practitioners from both disciplines have cultivated a profound understanding of their respective principles and techniques, which sets each style apart. If you have the opportunity to explore either style in your locality,

consider giving both a try. This approach allows you to determine which style resonates more with you. Additionally, it's advisable to explore various schools, as factors like the school's environment, instructor expertise,

and other dynamics could also play a significant role in shaping your decision.
